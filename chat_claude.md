The nav problem
"All Categories" as the only nav item means a user who's already browsed into a category has no easy way back, no context of where they are, and no next action. A directory nav needs to work at every level of the hierarchy — homepage, category page, listing page. So it needs:
Logo → Search (always visible, not just on homepage) → Location pill (showing their current area, clickable to change) → "Add your business" on the right. That's it. Four elements. The search bar being persistent in the nav on scroll is what transforms it from a static page into something that feels usable.

The location flow
This is the most important UX decision on the whole site. Here's how it should work:
On first visit — a subtle banner or modal below the hero (not blocking it): "Show businesses near you?" with two options: Use my location (browser geolocation API) or Enter your suburb (text input with autocomplete from your suburb list). Once set, it persists in localStorage. The nav location pill updates to show "Florida" or "Sandton" or whatever they selected.
On subsequent visits — location is already set, they see it in the nav immediately. No prompt needed.
The homepage then has a section: "Top rated near [Suburb]" — pulling the highest-rated listings across all categories within that suburb. This is dynamic (JS), not indexed — but it's the engagement layer that keeps users on the page longer, which indirectly helps SEO.

The homepage hero — what it actually needs
Right now your hero is doing zero work beyond saying the site exists. It needs to do five things simultaneously:
Tell Google what the page is about (H1 with location keyword). Give the user an immediate action (search). Orient them by location (suburb context). Signal trust (listing count, data source). Give them a category entry point if they don't search.
Structure:
[Eyebrow: Johannesburg · Gauteng · South Africa]
[H1: Find local businesses in Johannesburg]
[Subtext: 1,279 real listings sourced from Google Maps]

[Search bar: "Search plumbers, barbers..." | [Your suburb ▾] | [Search →]]

[Location banner if not set: "Show results near you →"]

[Category strip: 8 category text-links with counts]
Below the hero: Top rated near you (location-aware) → Browse by category (full grid) → Recently added (signals freshness to Google).

The category page — what's missing
Currently: header stats + suburb area cards + A-Z search. That's actually good structure. What it's missing:
A short paragraph of real copy above the suburb cards — not filler, but "There are 88 accredited K53 driving schools listed across 43 areas in Johannesburg and Gauteng." That sentence alone has 6 keywords Google will pick up.
A "Top rated overall" section showing the top 3 listings across all suburbs in that category — so users don't have to drill into a suburb to see quality results. This also creates more internal link targets.

The listing page — what's missing
The "About this area" blurb in the sidebar should be a proper paragraph generated by Groq per suburb/category combo — 3–4 sentences. It currently reads like a system message. Google reads sidebar content too.
A "Claim this listing" nudge below unclaimed listings — small, unobtrusive text: "Is this your business? Claim it free →" That's your monetisation entry point built into the page.
